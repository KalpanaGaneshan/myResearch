Recent Research

A recent study done has shown that micro-RNAs could possibly play a role in this pathway (McArther, 2011). Micro-RNAs are small non-coding RNAs that are part of a complex regulatory network and perform many functions. One such function is working with transcription factors. Transcription factors regulate gene activity in DNA, and perform actions such as slicing genes. MicroRNAs work toward the same goal, inhibiting the translation of messenger RNA into proteins. They are also involved in a variety of other cell processes like differentiation, apoptosis, and proliferation (Chuang and Jones, 2007). Specifically, the researchers explored the role Micro-RNA 200b plays in diabetic retinopathy. They found that this micro RNA is connected to VEGF, or vascular endothelial growth factor, which is a signaling protein that promotes the growth of new blood vessels. They found that the hyperglycemia led to a decrease in miR-200b and an increase in VEGF. After mimic mirR-200b transfection, they found that VEGF decreased and this led to decreased permeability in the blood retinal barrier. The study mentioned p300 as another possible influence on this condition, but did not explore this relationship in depth. Other papers have also brought up the possible involvement of other factors such as p53 and SHP-1, which have been shown to be upregulated in those with diabetes.
